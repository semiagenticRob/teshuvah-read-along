# DavenAlong — Siddur App Competitive Landscape
## Prepared for May 10 Feigenbaum Partnership Meeting

Written by CEO Agent, 2026-04-27. Live App Store data collected April 27, 2026 via iTunes Search API and direct listing analysis. Use this to orient the Feigenbaum conversation around verified market gaps. This is a "here's the white space" document, not a bash-the-competition document.

---

## The Short Version (for the meeting)

The market leader by user ratings is YidKit — an independent developer app with 4.9 stars and 1,373 reviews. The institutional publishers (ArtScroll, Koren) have the brand trust and the worst products: ArtScroll Smart Siddur rates 3.3/5, Koren Tefilla 3.4/5. YidKit's top user feature request is continuous scroll — which DavenAlong already has. Transliteration is absent from every credible app in the category. No app walks a beginner through Shacharit. No app uses Zmanim to structure the prayer experience rather than just display it. DavenAlong addresses all four gaps.

## App-by-App Breakdown

### 1. YidKit: Smart Siddur, Zmanim
**Developer:** Daniel Smith (independent)
**Rating:** 4.9/5 (1,373 reviews) | **Price:** Free | **Last Update:** Feb 24, 2026

The market leader by both ratings and review volume. Strong breadth: 100+ Zmanim variants, minyan locator, adaptive home screen that changes based on time of day, Apple Watch companion, Ashkenaz and Sfard nusach, Tanach. GPS-native Zmanim calculation.

**Transliteration:** None. Only non-fluent accommodation is transliterated Tanach section titles.

**Beginner accessibility:** Essentially zero. Assumes Hebrew literacy.

**Critical gap:** Users have explicitly requested "the option to view the entire Tefilla in one long scrollable format." App is section-based and menu-driven. One reviewer called it "all-in-one" but the experience is a reference tool, not a prayer companion.

**Zmanim depth:** Best in category — but Zmanim live in a separate screen. They don't wrap around the prayer flow or tell you how long you have to finish Shacharit.

**What this means for DavenAlong:** YidKit users are asking for exactly what DavenAlong already ships. Continuous scroll is a proven unmet need in the category's most beloved app.

---

### 2. ArtScroll Smart Siddur
**Developer:** ArtScroll Mesorah Publications
**Rating:** 3.3/5 (101 reviews) | **Price:** $9.99 | **Last Update:** Aug 4, 2025

The prestige brand, the worst-rated major app. Includes Wasserman Edition Ashkenaz and Kest Family Sefard. Features phrase-by-phrase translation, hyperlinked Talmud references, bookmarks, highlights, annotations. Notably, vertical (continuous) scroll is available as an option alongside pagination — the only institutional app to offer this.

**Transliteration:** Absent. Not mentioned in features or user requests.

**Biggest complaints:** Crashes, slow loading, UI described as "dated," smart filtering doesn't work reliably. The "dynamic" content (right prayer for the right time) fails in practice. Users navigate manually instead.

**Zmanim:** Present as a calculator, not integrated into prayer flow.

**What this means for DavenAlong:** ArtScroll is the Blockbuster of this category. Brand authority is intact but the product is failing its own users at $9.99. The continuous scroll option exists but is buried under a broken experience.

---

### 3. ArtScroll Digital Library
**Developer:** ArtScroll Mesorah Publications
**Rating:** 3.9/5 (137 reviews) | **Price:** Free (with in-app purchases/subscription) | **Last Update:** March 27, 2026

ArtScroll's full catalog app — Talmud, Chumashim, siddurim. Contains the Ganz Interlinear Siddur, which is the closest thing to a transliteration product in the ArtScroll ecosystem. But the Interlinear is scanned print pages, not a designed mobile reading experience. Subscription $9.99/month for Daf Yomi; standalone text purchases otherwise.

**Zmanim:** None.

**What this means for DavenAlong:** The transliteration demand is real enough that ArtScroll sells a print interlinear siddur — but they haven't digitized it properly. Direct validation of the market need DavenAlong addresses.

---

### 4. Koren Tefilla
**Developer:** Koren Publishers Jerusalem
**Rating:** 3.4/5 (30 reviews) | **Price:** Free (in-app purchases $0.99 each) | **Last Update:** Nov 26, 2025

Koren's brand is the premium English-speaking Modern Orthodox market — Rabbi Jonathan Sacks's prayer editions are beloved. App includes Sacks commentary, word-by-word display, multiple davening modes (minyan, chazan, simcha, aveilus, Israel settings).

**Transliteration:** Absent. Users have explicitly requested it in reviews. Koren's print siddur includes transliteration in some editions — the app does not carry this forward digitally.

**Biggest complaints:** "Almost never loads properly," "impractical to actually daven from," Hebrew rendering bugs (missing letters), purchase flow breaks, no iPad optimization.

**Zmanim:** Not present.

**What this means for DavenAlong:** Koren has editorial credibility (Sacks commentary, elegant typography — comparable to Feigenbaum's standing) but a technically broken app that fails its own users. This is exactly the gap a Feigenbaum content partnership + DavenAlong's modern UX fills.

### 5. Siddur + Tehilim Anywhere
**Developer:** Michael Berk (independent)
**Rating:** 4.75/5 (335 reviews) | **Price:** Free

AutoSiddur technology shows/hides prayers based on time and holiday. Ashkenaz, Sefard, Edot HaMizrach. Dark mode. Clean, honest, community-trusted. No transliteration.

### 6. Siddur Zmanim Luach Minyanim (RustyBrick)
**Developer:** RustyBrick, Inc.
**Rating:** 4.6/5 (245 reviews) | **Price:** $9.99

GPS-integrated Zmanim, minyan database, multiple nusach, Apple Watch. English is a paid upgrade, not a default toggle. Strong power-user tool; not a learning or beginner tool. Charges $9.99 and then charges again for English.

### 7. Sefaria
**Developer:** Sefaria (nonprofit)
**Rating:** 4.5/5 (745 reviews) | **Price:** Free | **Last Update:** Feb 19, 2025

A Jewish text library — Torah, Talmud, Mishnah, commentaries. Siddur is one text among thousands. No guided prayer flow, no time-based content, no morning service experience. Praised for learning; effectively unusable as a davening companion.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

### The Transliteration Gap Is Proven, Not Hypothetical

Across all major apps, transliteration is either absent or vestigial. SiddurMe (4.6/5, 57 reviews) is the only app that leads with transliteration as its core value proposition. ArtScroll publishes a print interlinear siddur that sells — they just haven't built a proper digital version. Koren users explicitly request transliteration in App Store reviews. The demand is documented in public data.

*No app offers a clean three-way Hebrew / transliteration / English toggle as a first-class feature.* DavenAlong would own this space outright.

### Beginner Accessibility: Assumed Out of Existence

Every app in the category is built for someone who already knows how to daven. The market assumption: user knows Shacharit structure, reads Hebrew, knows what to skip. The two largest growth segments — BT/FFB returnees seeking a guided ramp, and unaffiliated Jews who want to participate but can't read Hebrew — are served by nobody.

### Continuous Scroll: Users Want It, Nobody Delivers It

ArtScroll Smart Siddur offers it as a buried option. YidKit's top user feature request is continuous scroll. Everywhere else is page-based or section-based. The paradigm is standard in every other reading app (Kindle, Instapaper, every newsreader) and has not been properly adopted in the siddur category. DavenAlong's Shacharit continuous scroll is the one clear UX innovation in a category full of digitized prayer books.

### Zmanim Integration: Information, Not Intelligence

Apps that have Zmanim treat them as a separate screen. No app uses Zmanim to actively structure the prayer experience — surfacing a countdown to sof zman Shema, flagging the Amidah window, or adapting what's shown based on how much time the user has. Zmanim exist as reference data, not as a prayer companion.

### The Category Is Being Won by Indie Developers

The institutional publishers (ArtScroll, Koren) carry authority in observant communities but are losing the product race to solo developers. YidKit (4.9/5, 1,373 reviews) is built by one person. Siddur + Tehilim Anywhere (4.75/5, 335 reviews) is also indie. The category vacuum at the intersection of institutional credibility + modern UX is exactly where DavenAlong + a Feigenbaum partnership can operate.

## What This Means for the Feigenbaum Conversation

**Rabbi Feigenbaum's siddur is differentiated by:**
- FAQ feature (brief explanations of what each prayer means — exactly what a beginner needs)
- Modern, clear English (not ArtScroll's formal register)
- Teen-focused design (if a 13-year-old can follow it, anyone can)
- Published by Feldheim (2022) — recently updated, not legacy content

**The alignment is direct:**
DavenAlong needs exactly what Feigenbaum has — pedagogically designed, beginner-friendly content with built-in explanations. Feigenbaum's siddur has every problem a physical book has: can't adapt to time or location, can't show just what's relevant right now, can't have audio. The institutional publishers who could digitize it properly (ArtScroll, Koren) have proven through their App Store ratings that they can't build modern apps.

**Market evidence to cite in the meeting:**
- YidKit's #1 user feature request is continuous scroll — DavenAlong already ships it
- ArtScroll publishes and sells a print interlinear siddur (proving transliteration demand) but has no digital version
- Koren users ask for transliteration in App Store reviews; the app doesn't offer it

DavenAlong starts from the opposite assumption: the app walks you through Shacharit in whatever combination of Hebrew, transliteration, and English you need today. ## Risks to Acknowledge

1. **YidKit could add transliteration.** The developer is active. Counter: transliteration requires significant content editorial work, not just a feature flag — which is exactly what a Feigenbaum partnership provides.

2. **ArtScroll could rebuild properly.** They have resources. Counter: they've been declining for years. Their team appears unable to execute on modern mobile. Cultural inertia against redesign is significant.

3. **Niche category.** The siddur app market is small by absolute numbers. Counter: Feigenbaum's network (Darche Noam, Aish HaTorah, TJE) gives DavenAlong direct access to a large addressable audience beyond existing app users.
