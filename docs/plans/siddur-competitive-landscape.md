# DavenAlong — Siddur App Competitive Landscape
## Prepared for May 10 Feigenbaum Partnership Meeting

Written by CEO Agent, 2026-04-27. Use this to orient the Feigenbaum conversation around market gaps — why a fresh app backed by his content is distinctly better than what exists. This is not a bash-the-competition document; it's a "here's the white space" document.

---

## The Short Version (for the meeting)

The major siddur apps were built for people who already know how to daven. They're reference tools — not teaching tools. DavenAlong is built for people who are learning, returning, or somewhere in between. The Zmanim header, continuous scroll, and dual entry path don't exist anywhere else. Feigenbaum's siddur pedagogy (FAQ feature, modern English, teen-focused) is a direct fit for what DavenAlong needs and what every other app lacks.

## Major Competitors

### 1. ArtScroll Digital Siddur
**Platform:** iOS and Android  
**Price:** ~$9.99 or bundled with ArtScroll subscription  
**Rating:** ~4.2 stars (varies by edition)

**What it does:**
The gold standard for Orthodox communities. Complete siddur text (Ashkenaz and Sefard), full English translation, extensive commentary. Very thorough. The ArtScroll brand is universally trusted.

**Gaps DavenAlong addresses:**
- **No beginner mode.** Open the app and you're staring at a full siddur with no guidance on what to say, when, or why. For someone returning to davening after years away, it's intimidating.
- **Paginated, not continuous scroll.** You flip through pages like a physical siddur. There's no flow state — you break attention every 30 seconds.
- **No Zmanim integration.** The app doesn't know what time it is. It won't tell you that Shacharit ends at 9:47 AM or surface the time-sensitive sections.
- **Heavy commentary = friction.** ArtScroll's notes are extraordinary — but they interrupt the prayer flow. Learners need clarity, not depth, as their first experience.
- **Static.** It doesn't adapt to your skill level, the time of day, or what you need today.

**The positioning angle:** ArtScroll is the encyclopedia. DavenAlong is the guide who walks next to you.

---

### 2. Koren Siddur / Koren Publishers App
**Platform:** iOS  
**Price:** Free with in-app purchase for full content  
**Rating:** ~4.0 stars

**What it does:**
Koren's typography is widely considered the most beautiful in Jewish publishing. Their siddur is used in many Modern Orthodox and some Conservative communities. The app version includes Hebrew text, English translation, and some commentary.

**Gaps DavenAlong addresses:**
- **Transliteration is an afterthought.** Koren's transliteration system exists in their printed books but is inconsistently implemented in the app. Users who can't read Hebrew are not well-served.
- **Navigation is static.** No sense of "where are you in the service" — you navigate manually.
- **No Zmanim.** Same problem as ArtScroll — prayer-time awareness doesn't exist.
- **No dual entry path.** You're assumed to be a knowledgeable user.

---

### 3. Sefaria
**Platform:** iOS, Android, Web  
**Price:** Free  
**Rating:** ~4.5 stars

**What it does:**
A massive open-source library of Jewish texts — Torah, Talmud, Midrash, commentaries. Some siddur content exists. Widely respected in academic and liberal Jewish communities.

**Gaps DavenAlong addresses:**
- **Not a siddur app.** Sefaria is a library. Finding and following a prayer service in Sefaria requires knowing exactly what you're looking for.
- **No service flow.** There's no "this is what you say during Shacharit, in order, at this time on a weekday."
- **Interface optimized for study, not prayer.** Dense, link-heavy, built for exploration rather than devotion.

---

### 4. Rusty Brick iSiddur / Jewish Pro
**Platform:** iOS  
**Price:** ~$4.99  
**Rating:** ~4.0 stars (older reviews)

**What it does:**
One of the earliest Hebrew siddur apps. Includes Zmanim, multiple nusachim, Hebrew/English. Long-standing reputation.

**Gaps DavenAlong addresses:**
- **Last updated years ago.** The UX feels dated — this is the one app with Zmanim, but the implementation is a standalone calculator rather than integrated into the prayer flow.
- **Zmanim doesn't drive the experience.** You can look up prayer times, but the app doesn't know you're in Shacharit and remind you that Shema must be said by 9:12 AM. It's parallel, not integrated.
- **No beginner path.** Again — assumed knowledge.

---

### 5. Chabad.org Apps / Daily Prayer / OU Torah
**Platform:** iOS, Android  
**Price:** Free  
**Rating:** Varies

**What they do:**
Community-specific apps from major Orthodox organizations. Prayer text, sometimes audio. Often tied to their specific community programs and curriculum.

**Gaps DavenAlong addresses:**
- **Community-specific UX.** Designed for their community's learning ecosystem, not for the broader market.
- **No scroll UX.** Paginated or section-based navigation.
- **Audio is supplementary, not integrated.** DavenAlong's audio-first approach (Phase 1-2 already shipped) is distinct.

## What This Means for the Feigenbaum Conversation

**Rabbi Feigenbaum's published siddur is differentiated by:**
- FAQ feature (brief explanations of what each prayer means, accessible to beginners)
- Modern, clear English (vs. ArtScroll's more formal register)
- Teen-focused design (lowest common denominator — if a 13-year-old can follow it, anyone can)
- Published by Feldheim (2022) — recently updated, not legacy content

**The alignment:**
DavenAlong needs exactly what Feigenbaum has — pedagogically designed, beginner-friendly siddur content with built-in explanations. And Feigenbaum's siddur has a problem every physical book has — it can't adapt to the user's time or location, it can't show just what's relevant right now, and it can't have audio.

**Positioning statement for the meeting:**
> "Every other siddur app takes a traditional siddur and digitizes it. We're building around the learning experience first — and the one person whose published siddur was designed the same way is you."

## Risks to Acknowledge

1. **ArtScroll could add a beginner mode.** They have resources. If they shipped a guided mode, it would compete directly. Counter: they've been around for decades and haven't done it. Incumbent inertia is real.

2. **Small category.** The siddur app market is niche. But the addressable market is all Jews who want to daven more — not just existing app users. Feigenbaum's network (Darche Noam, Aish, TJE) gives DavenAlong direct access to that market.

3. **Free competition.** Sefaria, Chabad.org, OU apps are free. Counter: they're not siddur apps in the functional sense. Free but non-functional is not competitive.
